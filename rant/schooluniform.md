# The Way School Uniforms Work is Garbage

## School uniforms

Everyone knows what school uniforms are, so I'm not going to explain that. I'm
mainly speaking from personal experience and reasoning when I say that the way
school uniforms are often implemented sucks. They don't *need* to suck, but they
almost always do.

But first off, how *are* school uniforms implemented? Well, at my school (which
provides facilities for kindergarten, primary, and secondary education), it
varies by your grade level. I have no idea how it works for kindergarten level,
but for primary it works like this: you have to buy the uniforms which are not
provided for free; girls and boys wear different clothes; PE and non-PE clothes
are different and you can only wear the "correct" uniform for the day (i.e. on
days with PE, you must wear PE clothes, and non-PE clothes otherwise). For
secondary, it works the same except girls and boys wear the same clothes on
non-PE days (still different on PE days).

## The thesis

The previously described implementation is an awful way to do school uniforms.

## The arguments

I will do this in a sort of "response" format. That is, I have - throughout my
time at the school - asked teachers and staff why the uniforms work this way,
and they have given me various fallacious explanations. And I will explain why
these justifications fail.

### "Uniforms promote equality!"

In theory, yes. In practice, abso-fuckin'-lutely not.

I think the idea behind this justification is that richer students cannot laud
their wealth over the poorer ones (although they still try to do this through
accessories, for which dress code rules are weakly enforced). The problem is
that "equality" is actually quite multifaceted. Economic equality is a large
factor (whether or not uniforms actually promote it), but so are many things,
and for my argument - so is gender.

If you wanted to promote equality, you would promote *all* equality. But our
school differentiates needlessly between "male" and "female" clothing. Why do
this? There genuinely is no need. Keep in mind, I'm not talking about
differences in fit or tailoring - which is fair - I'm talking about cosmetic
variations that have no impact on comfort whatsoever. Surely, if you cared about
equality, and the way you wanted to promote it was through making everyone's
uniforms the same, then you'd make everyone's uniforms the same; right? The fact
that we don't do this already invalidates the "equality" justification.

Not to mention, students who do not identify with or do not wish to conform with
the traditional gender binary are completely out of luck - there are no "enby
uniforms", only girl and boy uniforms. Obviously this places nonbinary or
otherwise gender-nonconforming students in an unsavory position.

Ironically, then, our uniforms don't just not promote equality - they actively
sabotage it and enforce discriminatory beliefs.

Also, I'm not even touching on the distinction between students having to wear
explicit uniforms and teachers having far looser dress codes (relatively
speaking); and the unhealthy power dynamic this implies. Maybe one day I'll
write about how the traditional teacher-student power relationship is horrific.

### "Uniforms reduce stress!"

The idea is that when students don't need to worry about picking out their
clothes in the morning, that they will be less stressed. The idiotism of this
should be obvious to anyone older than one year old, but let's unpack it anyway.

The extent to which picking out your clothes affect their stress depends on
effectively just two things:

1. How the clothes make you feel personally, and your attitude to wearing them
2. How judgemental your environment is and how likely they are to judge you
   specifically for wearing "unacceptable" or otherwise unfashionable clothes

Uniforms do not resolve the first. Now, instead of feeling shitty because you
chose clothes that make you feel wrong, you feel shitty because you were
mandated to wear clothes that make you feel wrong. It's actually worse, since
now it's actively coercive rather than the result of a bad decision. Also I
shouldn't even have to explain the negative psychological impact of forcing
explicitly gendered personal expression upon young minds.

Our uniforms also do not resolve the second issue. Finding oneself in a
judgemental environment is a much more fundamental issue than can be resolved by
enforcing a dress code. If a student's peers bully or make fun of them for the
clothes they wear, then introducing uniforms doesn't fix that whatsoever. Those
peers will find something else to make fun of, and it's utterly moronic to think
otherwise. Bullying is basically *never* a rational thing. A bully isn't just
gonna change their mind like that - can you imagine how odd it would be if they
did?

> Hmm, I wanted to tease you for your clothing, but it appears that would be
  hypocritical of me to do - so I will not. Say, as your bully, would you like
  to logically convince me of anything else? I am very willing to understand
  your situation.

... said *noone ever*.

### "Uniforms save time!"

This one is tangentially related to the previous argument.

The idea is that students spend a substantial amount of their time on getting
dressed, and that uniforms can reduce this. I cannot speak for all students, but
my friends and I basically never spend longer than 10-15 minutes getting dressed
to go out.

Still, let's assume the dressing time issue *genuinely is* an issue for some
people. How about, in that case, you make uniforms optional? Students who value
time more than wearing what they want can save time - and students who value
wearing what they want more than saving time can wear what they want. It's a
win-win. Even if the 10, 15, 20, 30(?) minutes it takes to pick out clothes and
get dressed are legitimately a problem - it doesn't follow that uniforms should
be enforced unilaterally, for *everyone* to wear.

And if you want to respond with something like "but then noone would wear
uniforms!", great. That's my point actually. The time spent on getting dressed
is so utterly inconsequential that noone would bother trying to reduce it.

Also, if the point was saving time, then teachers would also logically be forced
to wear uniforms. Despite the fact that their role is far more important than
that of the average student, and they actually *do* benefit from saving time, no
such uniform code exists for them. This fact alone disproves the "time save"
hypothesis - even the overworked people who actually need to save time don't
bother with the kinds of uniforms we have.

### "You need to get used to it!"

The idea here is that, since students will likely wear a uniform or formal
attire at their future place of work, that they need to get used to it early.
First of all, there's a very good change that they *won't* need to wear a
uniform at works, so we can challenge the premise at its root.

But even then, assuming that 100% of students will be made to wear uniforms at
their place of employment, that doesn't justify it. See, at work, you get
*money*. Fucking nobody wears their company mandated clothing because they like
it (except the bozos who think their employer has their interests at heart),
they wear those clothes because they are paid to do so as part of the job.

If you want to use this "get used to it" justification to rationalize making me
wear my uniform, how 'bout you fuckin' pay me for it? Oh - you won't? That's
cause its a terrible justification and not reasonably comparable to a job
situation.

### "Students all wearing uniforms creates a good school image!"

If you believe that the professionalism of a school, an abstract theoretical
thing, is more important than the comfort and personal expression of its
students, which are very real - then you are a shithead. Fuck you.

## Conclusion

I hope you see why these common justifications and defenses of school uniforms
are steaming garbage - the intellectual equivalent of having your head shoved in
a beehive and pretending post-hoc that this is actually a desirable state of
affairs.

The way our school does uniforms and dress code is, at best, worthless; and, at
worst, fucked up and discriminatory. This ought to change and uniforms ought not
be implemented how they currently are. Noone ever died over a uniform, but
stupid bullshit is stupid bullshit - and it must be avoided.

I Hate the Advertising Industry

What is an advertisement?

This question is more definitional in nature. For the sake of this written work, I will consider an advertisement as this: some piece of media produced either centrally (paid or unpaid) or for pay (centrally or decentrally) to promote a product or service to a decentral audience for the purpose of making a profit on that product or service.

As per this definition, an advertisement is a piece of media (video, written word, audio, etc.) defined against two axes, as follows:

-

Paid

Unpaid

Produced centrally

Advertisement

Advertisement

Produced decentrally

Advertisement

Not advertisement

I think this is an appropriate way to define the kinds of advertisements which we'll be considering, though it is too narrow to include all types of advertisement (e.g. door-to-door sales and telemarketing are colloquially types of advertising, but don't meet our definition as they are not media). Examples of advertisements, according to our definition, along with their respective quadrants, would be:

-

Paid

Unpaid

Produced centrally

Billboards, internet banner ads (produced by advertising agency*)

Billboards, internet banner ads (produced by advertising division*)

Produced decentrally

YouTube sponsorships, paying some random teen to promote your product on social media

-

*What I mean by this and the difference between an advertising "agency" and "division" will be elaborated on in the next section.

As well as production, there is also the part of the definition concerning spread to a "decentral audience". What I mean by this, is that the advertisement exceeds the broad "realm" in which it was produced. For example: a corporation won't produce an advertisement for its own personal use - just because they really like watching advertisements, they'll do it for promotion to other people (for the record, I'm aware of the idea of "internal marketing", but that is produced to reinforce an opinion, rather than create it. I.e., an advertisement is meant to create commitment for a product within a person, and internal marketing is meant to reinforce commitment in those who already have it).

To further give the idea of "exceeding the broader realm", we can take the example of the same promotional video in two contexts - placed on the website of the company who had it produced v.s. as a 30-second prefix to an unrelated YouTube video. Here, there are two "realms", defined mostly by our expectations for them. There is the company website realm, where we would expect to see the products and creations of the company; and there is the YouTube realm, where we expect to see YouTube videos. In the YouTube realm, it matches the "decentral audience" requirement, as we don't go on YouTube to engage with the company realm - but we do go on a company website to engage with the company realm; the YouTube realm is not part of the central company realm, so when the company realm spills over into it, it enters a decentral realm relative to itself, exposing itself to a decentral audience.

I want to reemphasize that this definition does not include all advertisements. But something which does fit our definition is very likely to be an advertisement, which is the benefit of these kinds of stricter requirements. And, either way, as a socioeconomic phenomenon, it's difficult to apply a one-size-fits-all definition to advertisements.

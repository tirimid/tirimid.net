I Hate the Advertising Industry

Preface

The latter part of this depends on some basic understanding of class analysis. I have omitted several arguably important points, and perhaps not argued everything as explicitly as I could have, but I reckon it's good enough.

Lemme at it!

Advertising is not, inherently, worse than any other capitalist sector. Indeed, it is partly a consequence of them rather than something essentially evil. Still, we feel something about advertisements and advertising in particular as soon as "annoying industries" are brought up. I want to note that I will talk partly about advertising in general, but I mainly mean advertising as a distinct specialization of labor.

Oil, gas, coal - these all pollute our airspace and kill our animalistic parts through physical means. Advertising, on the other hand, exists solely to pollute our minds and kill the human part of our existence; to substitute for our humanity an undying love for consumption. It perpetuates the kind of pseudo-individualism that noone needs - taking the beautiful concept of personalized expression and returning commodified slop as a function thereof.

And at least, at the very least, and for all their evils and excesses, fossil fuels actually do something. They are destroying the world, creating a soon-to-be uninhatitable hellscape; with millions already forced to evacuate their homes of many generations to escape ruin or death, but - at the very least - they actually give us some value. The fact that they should have long been replaced in favor of better means of energy generation doesn't negate the fact that they still give us that energy. But what does advertising do?

By my definition, an advertisement is a piece of media created with the specific purpose of encouraging purchase of some proprietary good or service, with the key factor that it is paid for by the owner / producer of that proprietary good or service. A simple recommendation made by your friend doesn't count as an advertisement, unless they have been paid for it in some way. Also, an advertisement should have some level of greater spread: i.e. the homepage for some company's website isn't really an advertisement, even if it is paid media designed to promote their suite of products - but a 30 second video they made to play before a YouTube video definitely is. The difference is that the homepage is localized to a space explicitly designated for that company, their website, while the YouTube advertisement is an intruder (however permitted and accepted) upon the domain of another. Both are equally accessible should you go looking for them, but only one of them sprawls beyond its bounds.

Nominally, advertisers connect interested customers to products which can favor them, thus mediating a win-win exchange. The customer gets something they want, the retailer of the product gets a sale, the manufacturer of the product gets a profit w.r.t. sales generated by the retailer, and the advertiser gets a payout from the manufacturer. And you know what? Let's take this seriously and think about it part-by-part. Not as a worthwhile investigation, rather a cursory look at things. This will be a critique of advertising in general, and then I'll dovetail into advertising as an industry.

First, I would question the notion of this supposed "interested customer". Let's say you were some random person, and you wanted to buy some or other product - as to say, you are an interested potential customer of such a product, whatever it may be. Now, which of the following would most closely match your state of mind?

  1. I will search for such a product. Perhaps, I will first check the stores closest to me - then maybe I'll go out a bit farther and see if any of these other stores have it. Maybe I'll go on Amazon or E-bay and search for the product if that's not possible. Or...

  2. Oh, gee! Golly, I thought I wanted this product real bad but it seems I haven't gotten any advertisements for it! Geez, looks like I won't be able to get it now...

Fucking obviously, not even the dumbest person you know would think in the second way. And why not? Cause you want the fuckin' product, that's why. If you really relied on an advertisement to figure out that you wanted to buy something, you aren't an "interested customer", you're easily led and should rethink your relationship to consumerism.

"But wait! What about products that may be useful to the average person, but are perhaps too technical for them to find out about through cultural osmosis, like VPN providers?". Okay, let's humor this question, although we should note that - at this point - we've actually backpedalled on our definition of an interested customer, from someone with preexisting interest, to somone who doesn't even have interest yet.

No problem, many people believe that an advertiser's role is to create this interest in the first place. So let's unpack why that doesn't make it any better.

Let's take the previous example - VPN providers. This should be familiar to practically everyone, seeing as our favorite YouTubers can't seem to shut up about them (I'm not blaming them personally, obviously I understand they rightfully want to make money. I'm talking about what they uphold by being forced into their position). Let's pretend that you've never heard of VPNs before, and you just saw a segment about SpyMalVPN on Mr.RacistGamingYTer's most recent video. This concept of a VPN is totally new to you, so what do you do next? Research further into the matter and find out whether you really want one Click the link in the description and enter the affiliate code, of course!

In this example, you are still no better than an easily led fool. Hey, if you do decide, after some short research, that you do actually want SpyMalVPN, good for you. But I could just as easily retort that there are much less degrading ways of learning about new things. And anyway, what's more likely: that you'll look into the matter and decide that SpyMalVPN, the first VPN you've ever heard of, is actually the best? Or that it's probably horseshit in comparison to the others - better products with a lower advertising budget? With the growing corporatization of the internet and how hard that makes it to find honest information, I suppose it's a 50-50.

Either way, in the event that you do buy an advertised product, you end up as one of the following:

  1. A fool with no monetary agency

  2. A reluctant consumer to whom the specific advertising provided nothing but a marginal benefit and could have more effectively been replaced by a short introduction to the general product category rather than an advertisement for a specific product (since you will seek out products you want on your own, with no need for an advertiser)

  3. Someone who would have bought the product anyway, and, thus, who hasn't been impacted at all by seeing the advertisement

And yet, advertising is oh so profitable! Why? Partly because the vast, vast majority of us fall - even if during temporary lapses of judgement - into the first category shamefully often. Due to various economic and sociocultural factors, our lives are increasingly dominated by the will of capital holders - who obviously have an interest in tricking us into buying useless dogshit from them. Hell, where I live, you can't really go anywhere without crossing paths with a billboard asking you to purchase some inane shit you really don't need. Needless to say, that, with the overall growing discontent of everyday life, wears you down over time.

But we're getting off topic, this rant is meant to be specifically about advertisers, not consumer culture in general.

So, then, let's ask one more question - what value do advertisers produce? We could answer this materially, and say "since labor went into the creation of the advertisements, they are embodied with value proportional to that labor". And, at this point, we have to ask a followup - since labor must be productive in order to create value, is the labor embodied in an advertisement productive? Well, as I've outlined above, advertising only marginally benefits the target audience in the best case scenario, and in all other scenarios it is useless or degrading.

Let's think about it another way. In a world where growing crops was unprofitable, we'd still have agriculture (as evidenced by human history prior to capitalism). In a world where building housing was unprofitable, we'd still have places to live (as evidenced, likewise, by human history prior to capitalism). In a world where running the post office or the trains was unprofitable, we'd still have public post and rail (as historically has been, and often is, the case). These things very obviously have value beyond their rates on the Free Market™, even if their values are not individually obvious until further examination.

But what about the advertising industry? Obviously people will want to recommend certain products or talk about the virtues of their own - that's not my problem here. People are entitled to talk about whatever the fuck they want, and I'm entitled to ignore them. When I say the "advertising industry", I refer to companies or corporations whose sole focus is creating advertisements for others.

Similarly to other fake "professions", like landlording or investing, advertising as a trade derives its supposed value not from our desire to see advertisements, but from capitalists' desire to multiply their money in as risk-free of a way as possible. We can't forget that capitalists, who are the ultimate clientele of advertisers, live on the M-C-M' circuit. We can pretend every now and then that this-or-the-other advertisement was "really good!" or "innovative!" or something else, but let's be honest. All in all, being advertised to by these megacorporations is nothing short of sickening - the theft of our personhood in order to falsely inventivize purchase, so that we may reclaim our stolen good through the high of consumption and the temporary increased status within consumer society. Capitalists fuck the world over, and then hire little underlings to trick you into paying them for it. They rely on you being a fool.

Industrial advertising founds itself not on the needs of worker customers, but on those of owner customers, and as such, caters to owning class interests - through the promise of ultimately increased revenue.

Therefore, advertising does not intentionally produce value, as the underlying value proposition is not in the media created by the filmmakers and writers employed by advertising agencies, but in the potential for money to come slimily crawling into your wallet. Basically, when you, as a capitalist, pay for an advertisement to be produced, you are not paying for the advertisement, but for its consequences. And that, along with what I previously mentioned, is why advertising is so profitable.

So, in short, that's why I fucking hate the advertising industry. They degrade us, degrade culture, and the only value they produce is unintentional and would be better spent in filmmaking or something else that actually benefits people. Paid advertising is useless at best, and usually damaging.

Think twice before you get played.
